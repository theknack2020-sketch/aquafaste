## 1. Daily Water Intake Recommendations

### 1.1 EFSA Guidelines (European Food Safety Authority, 2010)

The EFSA Panel on Dietetic Products, Nutrition, and Allergies published Adequate Intake (AI) values for total water intake (from drinking water, all beverages, and food moisture). These apply under **moderate environmental temperature and moderate physical activity levels (PAL 1.6)**.

| Age Group | AI (mL/day) |
|---|---|
| Infants 0–6 months | 100–190 mL/kg/day |
| Infants 6–12 months | 800–1,000 |
| Children 1–2 years | 1,100–1,200 |
| Children 2–3 years | 1,300 |
| Children 4–8 years | 1,600 |
| Boys 9–13 years | 2,100 |
| Girls 9–13 years | 1,900 |
| Adolescents ≥14 & adult females | 2,000 (P95: 3,100) |
| Adolescents ≥14 & adult males | 2,500 (P95: 4,000) |
| Elderly | Same as adults |
| Pregnant women | +300 mL/day above non-pregnant AI |
| Lactating women | +700 mL/day above non-lactating AI |

EFSA assumes ~20% of total water comes from food; therefore fluid-only recommendations are **1.6 L/day for women** and **2.0 L/day for men**.

**Source:** EFSA Journal 2010;8(3):1459 — "Scientific Opinion on Dietary Reference Values for water"
https://efsa.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.2903/j.efsa.2010.1459

### 1.2 IOM Guidelines (Institute of Medicine, USA, 2004)

The IOM set Adequate Intakes based on **median intakes observed in US national surveys** (NHANES). Unlike EFSA, the IOM did not use urine osmolality targets.

| Group | AI Total Water (L/day) |
|---|---|
| Children 1–3 years | 1.3 |
| Children 4–8 years | 1.7 |
| Boys 9–13 years | 2.4 |
| Girls 9–13 years | 2.1 |
| Males 14–18 years | 3.3 |
| Females 14–18 years | 2.3 |
| Adult males ≥19 years | 3.7 |
| Adult females ≥19 years | 2.7 |
| Pregnant women | 3.0 |
| Lactating women | 3.8 |

IOM noted ~80% of total water intake comes from beverages, ~20% from food. They concluded that healthy adults can use **thirst as a primary guide** for water needs, but this is unreliable for elderly and during exercise.

**Source:** IOM. Dietary Reference Intakes for Water, Potassium, Sodium, Chloride, and Sulfate. National Academies Press, 2004.

### 1.3 WHO Guidance

The WHO does not publish a single universal daily intake number but references the principle that **fluid reduction of 4% body weight causes a 20–30% decrease in physical and cognitive performance**. WHO clinical guidelines classify dehydration into three stages for treatment purposes (see Section 2). The WHO publication "Nutrients in Drinking Water" (2005, ISBN 92-4-159398-9) supports the ~2L/day recommendation for adults in temperate climates.

### 1.4 Adjustments by Weight and Activity Level

**Weight-based rule of thumb:** ~30–35 mL per kg of body weight per day for adults in moderate conditions.

| Factor | Adjustment |
|---|---|
| Exercise | +400–800 mL per hour of moderate exercise; more for intense/hot conditions |
| Hot/humid climate | +500–1,000 mL/day |
| High altitude | +500 mL/day |
| Illness (fever, diarrhea, vomiting) | Significantly increased; WHO ORS protocol for severe cases |
| Pregnancy | +300 mL/day (EFSA) |
| Lactation | +700 mL/day (EFSA) |

### 1.5 Population Compliance

A cross-sectional survey of 16,276 adults across 13 countries found that **~60% of men and ~40% of women fail to meet the EFSA AI for fluid intake**. Elderly individuals had a significantly lower probability of compliance compared to younger adults.

**Source:** Ferreira-Pêgo et al. "Total fluid intake and its determinants." Eur J Nutr. 2015. PMC4472938.

## 2. Dehydration: Symptoms and Stages

### 2.1 Clinical Classification (WHO-adapted)

Dehydration is classified by percentage of body weight loss:

| Stage | Body Weight Loss | Key Signs & Symptoms |
|---|---|---|
| **Mild** | 1–3% (children: 3–5%) | Increased thirst, slightly decreased urine output, dry lips, mild fatigue, slight headache |
| **Moderate** | 4–6% (children: 6–10%) | Dark urine, dry mouth, decreased skin turgor, headache, dizziness, rapid heart rate, impaired concentration, irritability, nausea |
| **Severe** | >6% (children: >10%) | Very dark/no urine, sunken eyes, extremely dry mucous membranes, confusion/delirium, hypotension, tachycardia, muscle cramping, potential organ dysfunction |

At **5–6% water loss**: increased tiredness, headaches, nausea, paresthesias.
At **10–15% water loss**: muscle cramping, dry/wrinkly skin, delirium onset, painful/decreased urine output, vision decline.
Losses **>15%** are generally fatal.

**Sources:**
- StatPearls: Adult Dehydration (NBK555956)
- Physiopedia: Dehydration
- MSF Medical Guidelines (WHO-adapted classification)
- Mayo Clinic: Dehydration Symptoms & Causes

### 2.2 Adult-Specific Concerns

Dehydration ranges from "mild thirst and weakness to severe issues, such as hypovolemic shock and neurological impairment." It is especially dangerous for older adults, critically ill patients, and those with chronic conditions. Prevalence in US older adults ranges from 17–28%.

Notably, the claim that "75% of Americans are chronically dehydrated" has **no scientific evidence** in the medical literature.

### 2.3 Key Warning Signs for App Users

Practical indicators to surface in the app:
- **Urine color** — pale straw = well hydrated; dark yellow/amber = needs more fluid
- **Thirst** — already indicates ~1–2% dehydration (unreliable in elderly)
- **Fatigue and headache** — early, common signs
- **Dry mouth and lips**
- **Dizziness upon standing**

## 3. How Hydration Affects Health

### 3.1 Cognitive Performance

**Key threshold:** Cognitive deficits become measurable at **≥2% body mass loss** from dehydration.

- Military studies (Gopinathan et al.) first demonstrated that short-term memory, numerical ability, psychomotor function, and sustained attention all declined at >2% body mass loss.
- A systematic review of 24 trials (Dube et al., 2022; 493 participants) confirmed that **>2% body mass loss is the consensus threshold** for significant cognitive impairment.
- Affected functions include: concentration, reaction time, short-term memory, mood, and accuracy in complex tasks.
- fMRI studies show that mildly dehydrated individuals require **greater neural activity** (more effort) to maintain the same performance level — suggesting the brain compensates but at higher energy cost (Kempton et al., 2011).
- Water accounts for approximately **75% of brain mass**.
- Children and elderly are especially vulnerable — hydrated children perform measurably better on working memory tasks; community-dwelling older adults show slower psychomotor speed when dehydrated.

**Key finding on mood:** The evidence for mood state changes (fatigue, tension, anxiety) with mild dehydration is more consistent across studies than pure cognitive metrics.

**Sources:**
- Masento et al. "Effects of hydration status on cognitive performance and mood." Br J Nutr. 2014. Cambridge Core.
- Zhang et al. "Effects of Dehydration and Rehydration on Cognitive Performance and Mood." Int J Environ Res Public Health. 2019. PMC6603652.
- Basso et al. "Water intake, hydration status and 2-year changes in cognitive performance." BMC Medicine. 2023.
- Adan. "Cognitive Performance and Dehydration." J Am Coll Nutr. 2012.

### 3.2 Energy and Physical Performance

- Even **mild dehydration (1–2%)** can cause noticeable fatigue and reduced energy.
- Physical performance declines are well-documented at ≥2% body mass loss, particularly endurance performance.
- Dehydration impairs thermoregulation, increases perceived effort during exercise, and can cause muscle cramping.
- The body's typical daily water output is ~2,550 mL (respiration: ~300 mL, insensible/sweat: ~450 mL, urine: ~1,600 mL, feces: ~200 mL). Exercise, heat, and humidity can increase this to 3,100+ mL/day.

### 3.3 Skin Health

- Skin is the largest organ and depends on adequate hydration to maintain elasticity and turgor.
- Dehydration reduces skin elasticity — the "skin pinch test" (delayed recoil) is a clinical dehydration sign.
- Chronic mild dehydration is associated with dry, less elastic skin.
- Consistent hydration supports cellular function and skin appearance.
- **Note:** The evidence for water intake dramatically improving skin appearance in already-hydrated individuals is limited; benefits are most clear when correcting an existing deficit.

### 3.4 Digestion

- Water is essential for digestion, absorption, metabolism, and elimination of metabolites.
- Adequate hydration maintains the mucosal lining of the intestines and supports regular bowel movements.
- Dehydration is a common cause of constipation.
- Pre-meal hydration can stimulate production of digestive enzymes and gastric juices, improving food processing.
- The hypothalamus controls both hunger and thirst signals, and these can be confused — drinking water before meals can help distinguish true hunger from thirst.

## 4. Optimal Drinking Patterns

### 4.1 Sipping vs. Gulping

**Consensus: Sipping throughout the day is superior to gulping large amounts.**

Key physiological reasons:
- **Better absorption:** The gut absorbs water more efficiently when not flooded; slow intake reduces rapid transit and electrolyte dilution.
- **Reduced "bolus response":** Chugging a glass of water triggers the body's protective mechanism — kidneys rapidly excrete the excess, resulting in high urine output with poor retention.
- **Less bloating:** Gulping causes more air swallowing, leading to bloating and discomfort.
- **Hyponatremia risk:** Very rapid consumption of large volumes can dilute blood sodium to dangerous levels (water intoxication).
- **Research finding:** When water is consumed slowly (sipping), urine excretion is **up to 6× lower** than with gulping, meaning the body retains significantly more fluid.
- **Kidney limit:** Kidneys can filter approximately **1 liter per hour** — exceeding this causes excess excretion.

**Sources:**
- American Physiological Society, "The Science of Hydration" (2021)
- PelvicSanity: Sipping vs. Gulping research summary

### 4.2 Timing Around Meals

- **Before meals (15–30 minutes):** Primes digestive system; may help with appetite regulation.
- **During meals:** Small sips are fine; research does not support the claim that drinking water dilutes digestive enzymes. Stomach adapts its secretions.
- **Avoid:** Gulping large amounts during meals (causes bloating and discomfort).
- **After meals:** Continue normal sipping pattern.

### 4.3 Timing Around Exercise

| Timing | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| **Pre-exercise** | 400–600 mL (16–20 oz) about 2 hours before |
| **During exercise** | 120–240 mL (4–8 oz) every 15–20 minutes |
| **Post-exercise** | Replenish with water + electrolytes based on sweat loss; ~150% of fluid lost |

### 4.4 Daily Timing Strategy

- **Morning (upon waking):** 1–2 glasses to rehydrate after overnight fast. Room-temperature water is gentler on the system.
- **Mid-morning → afternoon:** Consistent sipping; 30–40% of daily intake in the morning.
- **Pre-dinner:** Hydrate before evening meal; especially important if alcohol was consumed.
- **Evening:** Taper water intake 2 hours before bed to minimize sleep disruption.
- **Before bed:** Small sips only.

### 4.5 Practical Hydration Cues

- Urine color: pale yellow = well-hydrated; dark yellow = drink more
- Thirst: by the time you feel thirsty, you're already mildly dehydrated (~1–2%)
- Elderly: thirst mechanism becomes less reliable — programmed drinking is recommended
- General frequency: aim for 150–200 mL every 30–60 minutes during waking hours

## 5. Electrolyte Balance

### 5.1 Key Electrolytes

| Electrolyte | Role in Hydration |
|---|---|
| **Sodium (Na⁺)** | Primary regulator of extracellular fluid volume; drives water absorption in the gut; lost in sweat |
| **Potassium (K⁺)** | Primary intracellular cation; critical for muscle function and nerve signaling |
| **Magnesium (Mg²⁺)** | Supports energy production at cellular level; aids muscle relaxation; may improve sleep quality |
| **Chloride (Cl⁻)** | Works with sodium to maintain fluid balance; component of stomach acid |
| **Calcium (Ca²⁺)** | Muscle contraction, nerve function |

### 5.2 When Electrolyte Replacement Is Needed

- **During exercise >60 minutes** — especially in hot conditions
- **Heavy sweating** — sweat contains ~0.5–1g sodium per liter
- **After illness** (vomiting, diarrhea) — WHO recommends Oral Rehydration Salts (ORS): 75 mL/kg over 4 hours for moderate dehydration
- **Plain water alone is insufficient** during heavy fluid loss — can cause hyponatremia
- **Normal daily diet** typically provides adequate electrolytes for sedentary to moderately active people

### 5.3 Hyponatremia (Water Intoxication)

- Occurs when blood sodium drops below 130 mEq/L
- Caused by drinking excessive plain water without electrolyte replacement
- Symptoms: nausea, headache, confusion, seizures; can be fatal
- Most commonly seen in endurance athletes (marathon runners) who over-hydrate with plain water
- **Prevention:** Include electrolyte-containing fluids during prolonged exercise; pace water intake; do not exceed ~1 L/hour of plain water

### 5.4 WHO Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS) Composition

Standard WHO-UNICEF ORS per liter:
- Sodium chloride: 2.6 g
- Trisodium citrate, dihydrate: 2.9 g
- Potassium chloride: 1.5 g
- Glucose, anhydrous: 13.5 g
- Osmolarity: 245 mOsm/L

## 6. Caffeine and Hydration

### 6.1 Summary of Evidence

**The widely held belief that coffee/tea causes dehydration is largely a myth when consumed in moderate amounts.**

Key findings from published reviews:

- **Low-to-moderate caffeine doses** (standard servings of tea, coffee, soft drinks) show **no significant diuretic action** and do not cause fluid loss in excess of the volume ingested.
- **Large acute doses (≥250–300 mg, ~2–3 cups of coffee)** can produce a **short-term, mild diuresis** in caffeine-naïve individuals, but a profound tolerance develops in regular consumers.
- A crossover study with 50 habitual male coffee drinkers (3–6 cups/day) found **no significant differences** in any hydration markers between coffee and water trials over 3 days. Coffee provided "similar hydrating qualities to water."
- A meta-analysis found that caffeine-induced diuresis was **small in magnitude** (effect size = 0.29), **did not exist during exercise** (ES = 0.10), and caffeine dosage did not significantly influence the effect.
- Intake of **>600 mg/day** (~6+ cups of coffee) should be compensated with additional water intake.

**Practical guidance for the app:** Moderate caffeine consumption (3–4 cups of coffee/day, <400 mg caffeine) **counts toward daily fluid intake** and does not need to be offset by additional water.

**Sources:**
- Maughan & Griffin. "Caffeine ingestion and fluid balance: a review." J Hum Nutr Diet. 2003.
- Killer et al. "No Evidence of Dehydration with Moderate Daily Coffee Intake." PLOS ONE. 2014. PMC3886980.
- Armstrong. "Caffeine, body fluid-electrolyte balance, and exercise performance." Int J Sport Nutr Exerc Metab. 2002.
- Zhang et al. "Caffeine and diuresis during rest and exercise: A meta-analysis." PMC4725310.
- Seal et al. "Coffee with High but Not Low Caffeine Content Augments Fluid and Electrolyte Excretion at Rest." PMC5563313.

### 6.2 Dose-Response Summary

| Caffeine Dose | Hydration Impact |
|---|---|
| <200 mg/day (1–2 cups coffee) | No measurable diuretic effect; fully hydrating |
| 200–400 mg/day (2–4 cups) | Minimal diuretic effect in habitual consumers; fluid retained |
| >400–600 mg/day (4–6 cups) | Mild diuresis possible; compensate with modest extra water |
| >600 mg/day (6+ cups) | Noticeable diuretic effect; active compensation needed |

## 7. Alcohol and Hydration

### 7.1 Mechanism

Alcohol suppresses the release of antidiuretic hormone (ADH/vasopressin), reducing the kidneys' ability to reabsorb water, leading to increased urine production.

### 7.2 Key Data

- Alcohol's diuretic effect causes water loss estimated at **~10 mL per gram of alcohol consumed**.
- Individuals with usual intakes of **>50g alcohol/day** (~4–5 standard drinks) need to actively compensate for the dehydrating effect.
- **Beer and wine in moderate amounts** do still contribute net fluid — "You can drink a beer and you will probably lose a little bit of it, but it's not enough to affect your hydration" (Tamara Hew-Butler, PhD, Wayne State University).
- However, the net hydrating effect of alcoholic beverages is lower than water due to diuresis.
- Higher alcohol concentrations (spirits) have a more pronounced dehydrating effect than lower-concentration drinks (beer).

### 7.3 Practical Guidance for the App

- For every standard alcoholic drink, suggest drinking **1 glass of water (250 mL)** alongside or after.
- Heavy drinking sessions should trigger a hydration reminder the following morning.
- Alcohol consumption before bed significantly increases overnight dehydration.
- Track alcohol intake as a negative modifier on daily hydration score.

**Sources:**
- Stookey. "The diuretic effects of alcohol and caffeine and total water intake misclassification." Eur J Epidemiol. 1999.
- American Physiological Society, "The Science of Hydration." 2021.

## 8. Key Metrics for AquaFaste App

Based on this research, the app should track and use these science-backed parameters:

### Intake Calculation Inputs
1. **Age** → age-specific AI tables (EFSA/IOM)
2. **Sex** → male/female AI differences
3. **Body weight** → 30–35 mL/kg baseline
4. **Activity level** → sedentary, moderate, active, intense (+400–800 mL/hr of exercise)
5. **Climate/temperature** → hot weather adjustment
6. **Pregnancy/lactation** → +300/+700 mL
7. **Caffeine intake** → no deduction at <400 mg; mild deduction above
8. **Alcohol intake** → deduction (~10 mL water loss per gram alcohol)

### Drinking Pattern Recommendations
- Distribute intake evenly across waking hours
- Target 150–250 mL every 30–60 minutes (sipping, not gulping)
- Pre-hydrate before exercise (400–600 mL, 2 hours before)
- Sip during exercise (120–240 mL every 15–20 min)
- Morning hydration priority (1–2 glasses upon waking)
- Taper 2 hours before bed

### Dehydration Alerts
- Urine color self-check reminders
- Long gap without water logged (>2 hours)
- Hot weather / exercise day reminders
- Post-alcohol hydration reminders
- Elderly user mode with programmed drinking schedule

### Electrolyte Guidance
- After >60 min exercise: suggest electrolyte drink
- After illness (vomiting/diarrhea): suggest ORS-type solution
- Normal conditions: plain water is sufficient
