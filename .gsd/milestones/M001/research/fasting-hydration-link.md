## 1. The Fasting–Hydration Connection (Science)

### 1.1 Why Fasting Increases Dehydration Risk

Dehydration is one of the most common and under-recognized side effects of intermittent fasting. Three physiological mechanisms drive this:

1. **Natriuresis of fasting** — When food intake stops, insulin levels drop. Insulin normally signals the kidneys to retain sodium. Without it, the kidneys excrete sodium rapidly (natriuresis), pulling water with it. Research published in *Metabolism* (1966) found sodium excretion rises starting on day 2 of a fast, peaks around day 4, then gradually subsides. The *American Journal of Medicine* (1971) confirmed potassium excretion is also rapid in early fasting (~10–15 mEq/day ongoing). During the first 24 hours, individuals can lose 1,100–5,700 mg of sodium above normal excretion.

2. **Glycogen-bound water release** — Each gram of glycogen stores 3–4 grams of water. As fasting depletes glycogen (first 4–6 hours), this water is released and excreted, creating a diuretic effect. This is why initial "weight loss" during fasting is mostly water.

3. **Loss of food-derived hydration** — Approximately 20% of daily fluid intake comes from food. During fasting windows, this source disappears entirely. Additionally, eating acts as a behavioral cue to drink — fasters often simply forget to hydrate.

### 1.2 Dehydration Risk by Protocol

| Protocol | Fasting Window | Dehydration Risk | Key Factor |
|----------|---------------|-----------------|------------|
| **16:8** | 16 hours | Moderate | Most of fast is overnight; miss ~1 food-based hydration cycle |
| **18:6** | 18 hours | Moderate–High | Glycogen depletion begins; natriuresis kicks in during waking hours |
| **20:4** | 20 hours | High | Extended natriuresis; only 1 meal to replenish electrolytes from food |
| **OMAD** | ~23 hours | High | Single meal cannot fully replenish electrolyte/fluid losses; sustained natriuresis throughout day |
| **Extended (36h+)** | 36+ hours | Very High | Cumulative electrolyte depletion; medical supervision recommended |

### 1.3 Symptoms of Fasting-Related Dehydration

Common symptoms that fasters attribute to "hunger" are often actually dehydration/electrolyte imbalance:
- Headaches (especially frontal, mild-to-moderate)
- Brain fog / inability to concentrate
- Fatigue beyond normal fasting tiredness
- Dizziness
- Muscle cramps
- Irritability / mood swings
- Bad breath (reduced salivary flow + acetone from ketosis)
- Constipation (dehydration-induced)
- Dark urine

### 1.4 The Electrolyte Dimension

Hydration during fasting is not just about water volume — it's about electrolyte balance:

- **Sodium**: Most critical loss during fasting. Target ~2,000–5,000 mg/day during extended fasts. For 16:8, electrolytes mid-morning + with first meal are recommended.
- **Potassium**: 1,000–3,000 mg/day for extended fasts. Available from avocados, spinach, bananas during eating window.
- **Magnesium**: 300–500 mg/day. Supports muscle/nerve function, mitigates "keto flu."

**Critical insight**: Overdrinking plain water without electrolytes can cause **hyponatremia** (dangerously low blood sodium), which shares symptoms with dehydration. This is a real risk for fasters who try to "drink through" hunger pangs.

### 1.5 What Can Be Consumed During Fasting Without Breaking the Fast

- Plain water ✅
- Sparkling/mineral water ✅
- Black coffee (no milk/sugar) ✅
- Unsweetened tea ✅
- Lemon water (zero-calorie) ✅
- Sugar-free electrolyte supplements ✅
- Calorie-containing beverages ❌

## 2. Hydration Recommendations by Fasting Protocol

### 2.1 General Baseline

Experts recommend 30–35 mL of water per kg of body weight per day as a starting point. For a 70 kg person, that's roughly 2.1–2.5 liters. General guidelines suggest ~15.5 cups (3.7L) for men and ~11.5 cups (2.7L) for women total daily fluid. During fasting, since ~20% normally comes from food, beverage intake must increase to compensate.

### 2.2 Protocol-Specific Strategies

#### 16:8 (e.g., noon–8pm eating window)
- **Morning (fasting)**: Glass of water on waking. Optional: black coffee/tea. Electrolyte supplement mid-morning.
- **Eating window**: Emphasize water-rich foods (fruits, vegetables, soups). Continue drinking water with meals.
- **Evening (transition to fast)**: Herbal tea. Moderate water before bed.
- **Electrolyte timing**: Mid-morning during fast + with first meal.

#### 20:4 (e.g., 2pm–6pm eating window)
- **Morning through early afternoon (fasting)**: Consistent water intake every 1–2 hours. Electrolyte supplement 2x during fast (e.g., morning + early afternoon).
- **Eating window**: Nutrient-dense, water-rich meals. Hydrating foods are critical in the short window.
- **Post-window**: Continue hydrating before sleep.
- **Electrolyte timing**: Morning + midday during fast; with meal.

#### OMAD (~1 hour eating window)
- **23-hour fast**: Water throughout the day. Electrolyte supplements 2–3x distributed through the fast. Sip electrolyte water every 2–3 hours during extended fasting hours.
- **Single meal**: Must include electrolyte-rich foods (leafy greens, avocado, nuts, seeds). Hydrate well before/during/after meal.
- **Electrolyte timing**: Morning, midday, late afternoon during fast; with meal.

### 3.2 Key Market Gap

**No app currently offers hydration tracking that is deeply integrated with and adapts to the specific fasting protocol being followed.** The opportunity:

1. **Protocol-aware hydration goals** — A 16:8 faster needs different hydration timing than OMAD. No app adjusts water goals based on fasting schedule.
2. **Electrolyte tracking** — No fasting app tracks sodium/potassium/magnesium intake or provides protocol-specific electrolyte guidance.
3. **Fasting-phase hydration intelligence** — No app explains *why* you need more water at hour 14 vs hour 4 (glycogen depletion, natriuresis).
4. **Dehydration risk alerts** — No app warns you when your hydration is falling behind relative to your fasting phase.
5. **Hydration-first UX** — Every competitor treats hydration as a secondary add-on to fasting. None make hydration the primary lens.
